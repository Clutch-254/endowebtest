What is Endometriosis?

Endometriosis is a condition where tissue similar to the lining of the uterus (the endometrium) grows outside the uterus, commonly on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, and tissues lining the pelvis. This tissue acts just like regular endometrial tissue â€” it builds up, breaks down, and bleeds with each menstrual cycle. However, because it has no way to leave the body, it becomes trapped, leading to pain, inflammation, and the formation of scar tissue.

Affecting approximately 1 in 10 women and individuals assigned female at birth during their reproductive years, endometriosis is a significant but often underdiagnosed health concern. The average delay in diagnosis is 7-10 years from the onset of symptoms.

Common Symptoms

Symptoms of endometriosis can vary widely among individuals. Some people may experience severe symptoms while others have minimal or no symptoms.

  • Painful periods (dysmenorrhea)
  • Pain during or after sexual intercourse
  • Pain with bowel movements or urination
  • Excessive bleeding during periods or between periods
  • Infertility or difficulty getting pregnant
  • Fatigue
  • Digestive issues like diarrhea, constipation, or bloating, especially during menstruation
  • Lower back and abdominal pain

Note: The severity of pain doesn't necessarily indicate the extent or stage of the condition. Even mild endometriosis can cause severe pain, while advanced endometriosis might cause little or no pain.

Frequently Asked Questions

What causes endometriosis?

The exact cause of endometriosis is not fully understood, but several theories exist:

  • Retrograde menstruation: Menstrual blood flows back through the fallopian tubes into the pelvic cavity.
  • Genetic factors: Endometriosis can run in families.
  • Immune system issues: Problems with the immune system may prevent the body from recognizing and destroying endometrial tissue outside the uterus.
  • Hormone imbalances: Estrogen promotes endometriosis.
  • Surgical scarring: Endometrial cells may attach to a surgical incision after procedures like a C-section.
How is endometriosis diagnosed?

The only definitive way to diagnose endometriosis is through laparoscopy, a minimally invasive surgical procedure where a surgeon looks inside the abdomen with a camera. However, healthcare providers often use a combination of methods:

  • Detailed medical history and symptom review
  • Pelvic examination
  • Ultrasound or MRI imaging
  • Laparoscopy with tissue biopsy (the gold standard)
How is endometriosis treated?

Treatment options vary depending on the severity of symptoms, whether you want to become pregnant, and how the condition affects your quality of life:

  • Pain medication: Over-the-counter pain relievers like NSAIDs
  • Hormone therapy: Birth control pills, progestins, gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonists and antagonists
  • Conservative surgery: Removing endometriosis implants while preserving the uterus and ovaries
  • Hysterectomy: In severe cases where other treatments haven't worked
  • Complementary approaches: Dietary changes, acupuncture, physical therapy, stress management
Can I get pregnant if I have endometriosis?

While endometriosis can affect fertility, many people with the condition can still conceive naturally. About 30-40% of women with endometriosis may experience difficulties with fertility. The relationship between endometriosis and fertility can involve:

  • Distorted pelvic anatomy due to scarring
  • Impaired egg release or development
  • Altered immune function affecting implantation
  • Poor egg quality
  • Compromised tubal function

If you're having difficulty conceiving, fertility treatments including medications, surgery, or assisted reproductive technologies like IVF may help. Early discussion with a reproductive endocrinologist is recommended.
